Title

What Actually Drives Vehicle Motion-State Classification? A Systematic Study of Temporal Encoding and Model Capacity in Graph Neural Networks

Alt.: “ET-NAGraphSAGE: A Temporal Extension of Neighbor-Aware GraphSAGE for Vehicle Motion-State Classification.”

Abstract

Classifying a vehicle's motion state — Stop, Lane-Change, or Normal driving — from the behavior of surrounding traffic is a core capability for driver-assistance and autonomous systems. Neighbor-aware spatial graph models such as NAGraphSAGE reason accurately about a single frame but ignore the temporal evolution of inter-vehicle kinematics, which is precisely what distinguishes a lane change from normal driving. We introduce ET-NAGraphSAGE, which augments neighbor-aware spatial aggregation with a temporal encoder over the past T frames of each vehicle's node features and of its neighbor-edge kinematics. Beyond the model, we present a controlled empirical study that isolates the true drivers of accuracy — temporal length, model capacity (channel width), spatial receptive field, and training budget — on the Gongeoptap roundabout dataset. Our best configuration attains 95.37 ± 0.45% State-Accuracy over four seeds, a statistically significant gain over the NAGraphSAGE baseline (94.07 ± 0.28%; Welch t=4.94, p=0.004), and 95.74% with a four-seed ensemble. We further find that capacity and receptive field are not additive — naively combining the two strongest factors regresses accuracy — and identify the Lane-Change↔Normal decision boundary as the dominant remaining error source.

I. Introduction

Understanding how a vehicle moves relative to its neighbors underpins collision warning, intention prediction, and cooperative planning. A practical formulation is motion-state classification: assign each vehicle, at each time step, one of three states — Stop, Lane-Change, or Normal driving. Graph neural networks are a natural fit, since a road scene is a set of interacting agents; recent neighbor-aware variants such as NAGraphSAGE achieve strong accuracy by encoding relative kinematics on graph edges.

However, such models operate per frame. A lane change is not an instantaneous configuration but a process: relative speed and lateral distance evolve over one to two seconds before the maneuver is recognizable. A single-frame model must infer this from a static snapshot, which we show caps Lane-Change accuracy at roughly 74% and, in turn, overall State-Accuracy.

We take two complementary steps. First, we extend NAGraphSAGE with a temporal encoder that compresses the past T frames of node and neighbor-edge kinematics into fixed vectors before spatial aggregation — ET-NAGraphSAGE. Second, and equally important for a rigorous conference contribution, we run a systematic ablation that quantifies which design factors actually move the primary metric. Rather than attribute gains to a single mechanism, we measure temporal length, channel width, receptive field, and training budget under identical data, budget, and evaluation, with multi-seed significance testing.

This study yields a model that significantly outperforms the baseline and, notably, several results that a "more-is-better" intuition would not predict. Our contributions are:

  • ET-NAGraphSAGE, a temporal extension that encodes node and neighbor-edge kinematic sequences and fuses them with neighbor-aware spatial aggregation.
  • A controlled empirical study isolating temporal length, capacity, receptive field, and budget, reported with mean ± std over four seeds and Welch t-tests against the baseline.
  • Three findings: channel width is the single strongest lever and has a sweet spot (h=192; larger hurts); capacity and receptive field are non-additive (their combination regresses); the Lane-Change↔Normal boundary is the structural bottleneck.
  • A model reaching 95.37 ± 0.45% (p=0.004 vs. baseline) and 95.74% as an ensemble.

II. Related Work

Vehicle behavior and state classification. [Brief survey of trajectory- and interaction-based methods; position motion-state classification as a per-agent, per-frame labeling problem.]

Spatio-temporal graph networks. STGCN, DCRNN, and AGCRN model traffic as graphs but treat edges as fixed distances or learned static weights, and target forecasting rather than per-agent state; we adapt representative baselines with a classification head for fair comparison.

Neighbor-aware aggregation. GraphSAGE and its neighbor-aware extension NAGraphSAGE encode relative kinematics on edges and use additive aggregation; NAGraphSAGE is the direct predecessor and internal reference for this work.

Temporal encoders. GRU/LSTM and recent selective state-space models compress sequences; we treat the encoder type as an ablated hyper-parameter (GRU adopted) rather than a claimed novelty.

III. ET-NAGraphSAGE

A. Graph construction. At each time step the scene is a graph whose nodes are vehicles with a 6-dimensional kinematic feature (position, speed, acceleration, heading). Each directed edge carries a 5-dimensional relative descriptor — relative speed, relative acceleration, longitudinal and lateral offsets, and Euclidean distance. Neighbors are selected by a hybrid policy: the K nearest vehicles within radius r. We use r = 20 m (average degree ≈ 5.7 on Gongeoptap), K₁ = 6 for the 1-hop and K₂ = 4 for the 2-hop neighborhood.

B. Temporal encoding (Stage 1). For each node i we form the length-T sequence of its node features and encode it with a GRU into hitemp. For each neighbor edge (i,j) we likewise encode the length-T sequence of edge descriptors into eijtemp. We use T = 10 (≈ 2 s). The encoder type (GRU/LSTM/Mamba) is selected by ablation; GRU is adopted for its stability and speed at this sequence length.

C. Spatial aggregation (Stage 2). The temporally encoded node and edge representations enter the neighbor-aware message-passing layer of NAGraphSAGE: each neighbor message combines the neighbor node embedding with the edge embedding, messages are combined by an additive aggregator, and a node-update MLP produces the new representation. We extend aggregation to two hops (K₁, K₂).

D. Output and loss (Stage 3). A single linear head produces the 3-way state logits. We train with cross-entropy and label smoothing (0.1); an optional KL-to-uniform attention regularizer is available but not required for the reported results.

IV. Experimental Setup

Dataset. Gongeoptap roundabout, 11 recordings, split into 619,951 / 128,855 / 135,869 train/validation/test samples (70/15/15). Labels are Stop, Lane-Change, and Normal driving; the set is imbalanced, with Lane-Change the minority class.

Protocol. All models train for 500 epochs (patience 150), Adam (lr 1e-3, weight decay 1e-4), cosine schedule, batch size 2048, mixed precision. We select the best-validation epoch and evaluate once on the test set. The primary metric is State-Accuracy; we also report Macro-F1 and per-class accuracy.

Statistics. Each configuration is run with four seeds (42, 846, 862, 995); we report mean ± std and a Welch t-test against the baseline. The NAGraphSAGE baseline is retrained under identical data, budget, and metric (94.07 ± 0.28% avg; 94.54% best run). All models use float32 inputs.

V. Results and Discussion

A. Main comparison (Table I). ET-NAGraphSAGE (h=192) reaches 95.37 ± 0.45% State-Accuracy, a +1.30 %p improvement over the baseline average (Welch t=4.94, p=0.0042) and +0.83 %p over the best baseline run. A four-seed softmax ensemble reaches 95.74% at no additional training cost, improving both Lane-Change (86.2%) and Normal (96.2%).

B. Temporal length (Table II). Under an identical h192 configuration, removing temporal context (T=1) drops accuracy to 92.45% and Lane-Change to 74.0%. Accuracy rises monotonically with history — 94.79% at T=5 and 95.37% at T=10 — and saturates near T=10, with most of the benefit already captured by T=5. This confirms that inter-vehicle kinematic history is what makes lane changes separable. Among encoders (Table II-b), GRU outperforms LSTM by 1.07 %p at this capacity and is adopted.

C. Model capacity has a sweet spot (Table III). Sweeping channel width gives 94.15 (128) → 95.37 (192) → 94.67 (256) → 94.44 (384). Capacity is the single strongest lever — the baseline's best model has ≈7× more parameters than our initial h128 model — but past h=192 accuracy declines, i.e. over-parameterization hurts.

D. Factors are not additive (Table IV). The two strongest factors in isolation are channel width (h192, K₁=6: 95.37) and receptive field (K₁=10, h128: 94.73). Combining them (K₁=10 × h192) yields only 94.49 ± 0.04% — below both — with Lane-Change falling to 82.0%. The very small variance shows this is a structural effect, not seed noise: the additional far/numerous neighbors introduced by K₁=10 interact with the extra capacity of h192 to degrade the Lane-Change boundary rather than help it.

E. Error analysis (Fig. 2). The ensemble confusion matrix shows Stop is essentially solved (99.8%), while errors concentrate on the bidirectional Lane-Change↔Normal boundary: 12.4% of Lane-Change samples are predicted Normal and 3.8% of Normal are predicted Lane-Change. Because the confusion is bidirectional, class re-weighting trades one direction for the other; separating this boundary requires better representation of the lane-change maneuver, which we leave to future work.

VI. Conclusion

We presented ET-NAGraphSAGE, a temporal extension of neighbor-aware graph aggregation for vehicle motion-state classification, together with a systematic study of the factors that drive accuracy. Our model reaches 95.37 ± 0.45% (p=0.004 vs. the NAGraphSAGE baseline) and 95.74% as an ensemble. The study yields three transferable lessons: model capacity is the strongest lever but has a sweet spot; capacity and receptive field are non-additive; and the Lane-Change↔Normal boundary is the dominant residual error. Future work — pursued in an extended journal version — targets edge-temporal dynamic gating, representation learning specialized to the lane-change boundary, and cross-road generalization on the DRIFT dataset.
